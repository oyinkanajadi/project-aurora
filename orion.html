01 — Executive Summary

A governance vacuum driving operational chaos

As operational complexity increased across services, the existing governance structures failed to scale. Decision-making became inconsistent, approval routes were unclear, and accountability was fragmented across teams with no single source of truth.

The result was avoidable delays, repeated escalations to senior leaders for decisions that should have been handled at team level, and a growing risk profile that threatened delivery timelines.

Project Orion was initiated to diagnose these governance gaps and design a structured, scalable framework — embedding clear ownership, defined approval pathways, and escalation models directly into operational workflows.

The output was not just a document. It was a living operating model: codified, communicated, and embedded into day-to-day practice across teams.

"Governance only works when people know what decisions they own — and trust that the structure will hold."

02 — The Challenge

Four structural failures creating system-wide risk

Process analysis and stakeholder interviews revealed consistent, recurring patterns across teams. These were not individual failures — they were symptoms of a broken governance architecture.

C-01

Unclear decision ownership. No single owner was defined for key decisions. Teams were either duplicating effort or avoiding decisions entirely, waiting for someone else to act.

C-02

Broken approval routing. Approval paths were undocumented and inconsistent. The same decision type was being escalated to different people depending on who was asked — or not escalated at all.

C-03

No formal escalation structure. When decisions stalled, there was no defined escalation path. Resolution depended entirely on personal relationships and individual initiative — not process.

C-04

High individual dependency. Critical process knowledge was siloed in individuals. When key people were unavailable, operations stalled. There was no resilience built into the governance model.

03 — End-to-End Process

From diagnosis to embedded operating model

The project followed a structured continuous improvement methodology — mapping the current state, defining the future state, co-designing the framework with stakeholders, then embedding and validating it in practice.

01

Discovery & Current State Mapping

Conducted structured interviews with team leads, operational managers, and senior stakeholders to surface governance pain points. Mapped all existing decision types, approval routes, and escalation behaviours — documenting the unofficial system that had evolved in the absence of formal governance.

02

Gap Analysis & Root Cause Identification

Analysed patterns across interview data to identify root causes behind repeated governance failures. Used a structured gap analysis to differentiate between process gaps, clarity gaps, and accountability gaps — each requiring a different design response.

03

Stakeholder Alignment Workshops

Facilitated cross-functional workshops to validate findings, challenge assumptions, and co-design the future governance model. Ensured that the people who would live inside the new framework had genuine input — building buy-in before the framework was finalised.

04

Framework Design: RASCI Model & Decision Thresholds

Designed a full RASCI ownership model covering all key decision types. Defined approval thresholds by decision complexity and financial/operational risk. Created a tiered escalation framework with defined trigger points, timescales, and named escalation contacts at each tier.

05

Embedding & Operationalisation

Embedded governance controls directly into existing operational workflows — not as a separate document to consult, but as a live part of how work was done. Trained team leads on the new model and established a governance review cadence to ensure the framework remained current.

06

Monitoring & Continuous Improvement

Established metrics to track governance effectiveness: approval turnaround time, escalation frequency, and re-work rates. Used data to identify remaining friction points and iterate the framework — treating governance as a living system, not a one-time deliverable.

04 — As-Is vs To-Be

Replacing informal convention with structured governance

The governance redesign required a complete architectural shift — not incremental improvements to a broken model, but a replacement of unwritten norms with a codified, accountable operating framework.

As-Is — Current State

  • ✕ Ad-hoc decision-making with no defined owner
  • ✕ Approval bottlenecks at senior leadership
  • ✕ No formal escalation path — resolved via relationships
  • ✕ High individual dependency creating operational fragility
  • ✕ Inconsistent documentation of decisions and rationale
  • ✕ No mechanism to surface governance failures systemically

To-Be — Future State

  • ✓ Defined RASCI ownership model for all decision types
  • ✓ Structured approval thresholds by risk and complexity
  • ✓ Tiered escalation framework with named contacts and SLAs
  • ✓ Governance embedded in workflows — not separate documentation
  • ✓ Decision log maintained with rationale and audit trail
  • ✓ Regular governance review cadence to maintain relevance
05 — RASCI Framework

Codifying ownership across every decision type

The RASCI model assigned clear accountability across five decision categories — eliminating ambiguity at the source. Each role is unambiguous: Responsible, Accountable, Supports, Consulted, Informed.

Decision Type Operational Lead Senior Manager Clinical/Tech SME Finance Director
Day-to-day operational decisions R A C I I
Process change & redesign S R C C A
Budget approval (within threshold) I R I A C
Escalated risk decisions C S C C A
Strategic direction & policy I C C C A
06 — Outcomes & Impact

Measurable improvement across governance metrics

The new governance framework delivered immediate and sustained improvements across four key dimensions — reducing friction, increasing accountability, and creating the structural conditions for scalable delivery.

50%
Faster Approval Turnaround

Structured approval thresholds meant decisions were resolved at the right level — removing unnecessary escalations and eliminating the bottleneck at senior leadership for routine decisions.

40%
Reduction in Operational Errors

Clear decision ownership reduced the ambiguity that drove repeated errors and re-work. Teams knew what they were responsible for — and acted accordingly.

+35%
Clarity in Ownership

Post-implementation stakeholder surveys showed a significant increase in perceived clarity — teams understood their role in the decision-making chain and trusted the governance model to hold.

Live
Scalable Governance Model

The framework was designed for growth — not a snapshot in time. The review cadence and living decision log ensure governance keeps pace with operational change across the organisation.

08 — Lessons Learned

What I would do differently — and what worked

1

Governance needs to be embedded, not documented

The biggest risk on this project was producing a well-designed document that nobody used. The investment in embedding controls directly into workflows — rather than writing a governance handbook — was what made the difference between adoption and shelf-ware.

2

Co-design is not optional — it's the mechanism for adoption

The stakeholder workshops were time-intensive and occasionally uncomfortable. But they were the reason the framework landed. People supported a model they had shaped — and challenged a model handed to them. Early investment in facilitation paid compound returns at implementation.

3

Start with decision typology before assigning ownership

On future projects, I would invest more time upfront in classifying decision types before assigning RASCI roles. The temptation is to map roles first — but without a clear taxonomy of decisions, ownership assignments become contested and imprecise.
